The New "Architecture"

What We Had

  • One prompt, autonomous execution
  • Parallel agents, specialized roles
  • Independent QA verification
  • 14 minutes, 3 products
  • Human kicks off, walks away

What They Left Us

  • One agent, sequential everything
  • No parallelism, no delegation
  • Agent checks its own work
  • Hours of babysitting for the same output
  • Human is the orchestrator now
Their replacement for multi-agent orchestration is you, sitting there, running prompts one at a time.

$100k/month isn't efficiency — it's brute-forcing with one agent what should be parallelized across several. The vendor gets paid more when the system is worse at autonomous work.

The Incentive Problem

  • Efficient orchestration: 14 minutes, ~$2 in tokens — bad for revenue
  • Sequential babysitting: 4 hours, ~$200 in tokens — great for revenue
  • Multi-agent parallelism is a cost optimization for users and a revenue reduction for vendors
  • The timeout isn't a technical limitation. It's a business decision.
When your vendor's revenue model is tokens-per-task, they have zero incentive to make your tasks finish faster.

This is not a bug. This is the #1 risk of building on AI platforms in 2026.

Platform Risk Taxonomy

  • Capability removal: Features you depend on disappear without notice — sub-agent timeouts
  • Silent degradation: Same API, worse results, no changelog — model swaps, guardrail changes
  • Pricing shifts: Your cost model breaks when token pricing changes — or when efficiency features are removed
  • Vendor lock-in: Your orchestration layer is coupled to one provider's agent API — no portable standard
  • Misaligned roadmap: They optimize for median users, not your use case — power users are collateral damage

What You Should Actually Build

Don't Build On

  • Vendor-specific agent orchestration
  • Sub-agent spawning as architecture
  • Any feature without an SLA
  • Assumptions about timeout/context limits
  • "It works today" as your test

Do Build On

  • Your own orchestration layer (bash, n8n, custom)
  • LLMs as stateless workers you call
  • Portable prompts, not platform features
  • Multiple provider fallbacks
  • Abstractions you control
Own your orchestration. Rent your inference.

The Survivable Stack

Your Layer — Orchestration
Bash scripts, n8n, custom engines — you own this, it can't be taken away
↓
Interface Layer — Prompt Files
Portable prompts, MANIFEST files — works with any LLM that can code
↓
Vendor Layer — Inference
Claude / GPT / Gemini / Local — swappable, interchangeable, disposable
The vendor is the most replaceable part of your stack. Design accordingly.

Surviving 2026

  • Record everything. Your demo works today. Screen-record it. Tomorrow it might not.
  • Own your orchestration. If a vendor can kill your workflow with a config change, it's not your workflow.
  • Treat LLMs as commodities. The model is not your moat. Your system design is.
  • Budget for platform breakage. Plan for 2-3 breaking changes per quarter. It's not if, it's when.
  • Keep your prompts portable. A good prompt works with Claude, GPT, Gemini. A platform-coupled one works with nothing when the platform changes.
